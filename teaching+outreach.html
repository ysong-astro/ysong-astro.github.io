Teaching & Outreach

Like many fellow grad students, I started off grad school as a teaching assistant and with zero prior exprience/training in teaching. In the past few years, I often looked for ways and practical resources that could better inform me on how to teach effectively and responsibly, outside of my usual TA duties. Most of what I learned through various experiences comes down to empathy and inclusivity. Effective teaching only comes after knowing who my students are, what they knew, and what they need. Below I listed some resources that I developed or gathered along the way. I believe that astronomy is for everyone. Aside from teaching, I volunteer at department public nights and with Dark Skies, Bright Kids! (DSBK), a UVa grad-led astronomy outreach organization for under-served elementary school students. As a member of the assessment team for DSBK, I help analyze the impact of our outreach clubs/camps and ways to improve them (check out our most recent publication here).

Resources

  • UVa Center for Teaching Excellence: many great resources on various aspects of course and classroom design, with newer ones focused on remote teaching.
  • Slides and handouts for a workshop I co-developed and co-facilitated on motivating non-major students in STEM classrooms from a TA perspective.
  • A very comprehensive resource guide on inclusive teaching from UMich, I especially appreciate this document listing various practical inclusive teaching strategies.
  • Sli.do: an awesome online tool to engage and interact with students during class without putting anyone on the spot to ensure a safe and equal learning environment for everybody. Another similar tool that I had great experience with is Socrative. Both are free to use with basic functions for small classes.
  • My lecture slides for UVa ASTR1220: Stars, Galaxies & the Universe
  • Slides for a talk I gave about telescopes at a public night event at the McCormick Observatory.
  • Scale of the Universe: A good way to introduce sense of astronomical scales to students without Astronomy backgrounds. Available in multiple languages!
  • Zooniverse: people-powered research projects for all ages and backgrounds, including the Galaxy Zoo and the Planet Hunter.
  • Stellarium: explore the night sky even on a cloudy day. Also can show constellations from various cultures.
  • Course Notes on a wide range of topics in Astronomy.
  • Some slides for a training session that I co-facilitated for new graduate TAs.
