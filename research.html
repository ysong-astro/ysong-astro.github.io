I am interested in understanding how galaxies change over time and their relationships with one another. (Ultra-)Luminous Infared Galaxies (U/LIRGs) in the local Universe are excellent places to start looking for some answers, as they are often galaxies going through violent transformations. We knew little about these systems until the first all-sky infared survey was carried out with the Infrared Astronomical Satellite (IRAS) in the 1980s. Their bright infrared emission comes from heavy layers of dusts heated by intense UV and optical radiation from massive stars and/or supermassive blackholes that are actively accreting surrounding materials. Deep optical observations later reveals that they are often in fact interacting or merging spiral galaxies. This process triggers bursts of star formation in these systems that can generate stars hundreds of times faster than the Milky Way, and along with it, large amounts of dust. The Milky Way is currently on its collison course with our neighbor galaxy the Andromeda, and most massive galaxies, near and far, go through similar major transformations. Detailed studies of U/LIRG in our local Universe can better inform us how galaxies evolve over cosmic time.

Why Radio?

Observations at radio wavelengths, like in the infrared, can more easily see through the layers of dust in the local U/LIRGs. Radio emission at high frequencies is also directly associated with massive stars, which makes for excellent tracers of star formation. With radio interferometors such as the Very Large Array (VLA) in New Mexico , and the Atacama Large Millimeter Array(ALMA) in Chile, we can see much finer details compared to existing infared observations.

The VLA equatorial GOALS Survey (VLA-GOALS)

The VLA-GOALS (PI: E. Avans) observed 68 out of the 202 local U/LIRGs in the Great Observatories All-sky LIRG Survey (GOALS) sample, at 3, 15 and 33 GHz with the VLA. These 68 systems all lie near the galactic equator (within +/-20 degrees in declination), and therefore are also observable with ALMA for molecular gas studies. A major goal of the VLA-GOALS is to study the deeply embedded star formation and AGN activity in these local U/LIRGs, and directly compare them to studies on local normal galaxies (i.e. spiral/disk galaxies). A study on star formation taking place in the outskirts of some of these systems has been published (Linden et al. 2019). A comprehensive description of the VLA-GOALS with data release including image atlas and photometric measurements is in active preparation and will be made available as part of my PhD dissertation.

Star Formation in Nuclear Rings

Rings of active star formation surrounding the nuclei of galaxies have been frequently observed in nearby spiral/disk galaxies as a result of slow, internal transformation within these relatively stable systems. With the VLA-GOALS, we were able to see star-forming rings that are deeply enshrounded in dusts at the centers of some local U/LIRGs (see image for an example: NGC 1614, left: optical image taken by the Hubble; right: our VLA observation of its nuclear ring). I am interested in knowing if and how these seemingly very similar features reflect the drastically different processes that normal spiral galaxies and U/LIRGs go through. We conducted a study comparing nuclear ring star formation in 5 local normal spiral galaxies and 4 local LIRGs, and we found that the most of the star formation in these LIRGs take place in their nuclear rings, which are also producing stars at least 10 times faster compared to the ones in the normal galaxies. A detailed description and discussion of the results of this study is submitted to the Astrophysical Journal and under active revision.
